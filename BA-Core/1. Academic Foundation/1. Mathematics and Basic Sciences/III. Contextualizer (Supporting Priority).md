## III.1 Principles of Macroeconomics
### 1. Overview
If Microeconomics is the "Logic of the Firm," **Macroeconomics** is the "Logic of the Ocean." You might be a great sailor (micro), but if a hurricane (macro) hits, your boat is still going down. Macroeconomics helps you understand the **External Environment**—the massive forces like inflation, GDP growth, and unemployment that a company cannot control but must survive.
#### Key concepts:
- `GDP & Economic Cycles:` Understanding when the "party is starting" (Expansion) or when it's time to "hide the snacks" (Recession).
- `Inflation & Purchasing Power:` Why $100 today won't buy the same amount of cloud server space in three years.
- `Fiscal & Monetary Policy:` How the government and Central Banks "tinker" with the economy by changing taxes or interest rates.

### 2. Application: Strategic Planning & Forecasting
As a BA, you use Macro data for **PESTEL Analysis**. If the Central Bank raises interest rates, your company’s cost of borrowing for a new project goes up. You need to forecast whether a long-term project will remain profitable if the economy slows down.

### 3. Recommended Resources
- `"Naked Economics: Undressing the Dismal Science"` by `Charles Wheelan`: (Again, because it's that good). The chapters on the Federal Reserve and International Trade are famous for making "boring" policy sound like a high-stakes thriller.
- `Jacob Clifford - Macroeconomics (YouTube)`: He wears a GDP t-shirt and uses a high-energy, comedic style to explain everything. He makes Macro feel less like a lecture and more like a game show.
- `The "Planet Money" Podcast (NPR)`: These are short, witty stories that explain the "hidden" economic forces behind everything from the price of t-shirts to the global supply of semiconductors.

## III.2 Mathematical Economics & General Economic Informatics
### 1. Overview
This is where the "Philosophy" of economics meets the "Hard Power" of mathematics and software. **Mathematical Economics** takes the theories you've learned and turns them into rigorous equations. **Economic Informatics** then takes those equations and puts them into a computer to simulate reality. It’s essentially "SimCity" for professional analysts.  
These subjects contains important concepts: Game theory, Input-Output analysis and `ERP & Simulation` (especially important for your semi-tech desiration).

### 2. Application: Advanced Modeling
You will use these skills to build `Predictive Models`. Instead of just saying "we might lose money," you build a simulation that says: *"There is a 15% probability of a loss if the exchange rate drops by 2 points."* This is the bridge to becoming a `Quantitative Analyst` (I think it's a career that can fit you, since `algorithmic trading and quantitative trading` is becoming a `continuous trend`).

### 3. Recommended Resources
- `"The Art of Strategy" by Dixit & Nalebuff`: A witty and accessible introduction to **Game Theory**. It uses examples from pop culture and history to show how math can predict human (and corporate) behavior.
- `Marginal Revolution University (MRU.org):` Their videos on "Game Theory" and "Development Economics" are visually stunning and often include quirky, dry humor.
