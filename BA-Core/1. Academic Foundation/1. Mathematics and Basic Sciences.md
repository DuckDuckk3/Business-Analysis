## I.1 Probability and Statistics:
### 1. Overview:
This is, undoubtedly, the `most important` subject in the entire pillar. Business Analysis is the art of `reducing uncertainty` to make `better decisions`. Without statistics, your perspective is just an opinion. You need this for data cleaning, hypothesis testing (A/B testing), and predictive modeling.
### Especially `hypothesis testing`, cuz it's a part of every business model. You'll also need probability to process raw data.  

For example, an e-commerce company wants to test whether a new checkout page increases the purchase rate.
They formulate the hypotheses:
- **Null hypothesis (H₀):** The new checkout page does not change the conversion rate.
- **Alternative hypothesis (H₁):** The new checkout page increases the conversion rate.  
The company runs an A/B test:

| Group | Visitors | Purchases | Conversion Rate |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Old page (A) | 10,000 | 420 | 4.2% |
| New page (B) | 10,000 | 510 | 5.1% |

After performing a statistical hypothesis test:
- significance level: α = 0.05
- p-value = 0.012
Because:
- p-value (0.012) < α (0.05) the company rejects the null hypothesis and concludes that the new checkout page significantly improves conversions.
  
This is hypothesis testing in business:  
`Companies` do `not` rely only on `“the numbers look bigger"`, but use `statistical evidence` (p-value, significance level, null hypothesis) to determine whether the improvement is likely real or just random chance.

### 2. Application: Predicting customer churn, A/B testing marketing campaigns, and quality control.
### 3. Recommend source: 
Since the knowledge taught in this subject at school is basically already sufficient, I think the things worth learning (which are not taught at school) are `hypothesis testing and multivariate random variables`, especially `under the assumption of unequal variances`.
### Multivariate Hypothesis Testing with Unequal Covariance Matrices

In multivariate analysis, when comparing the mean vectors of two independent groups ($\mathbf{\mu}_1$ and $\mathbf{\mu}_2$) without assuming equal covariance matrices ($\mathbf{\Sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\Sigma}_2$), we face the multivariate version of the **Behrens-Fisher problem**. 

The standard Hotelling's $T^2$ test is sensitive to violations of the homogeneity of covariance matrices. Instead, we use **Welch–James (WJ) Test** or a modified version of Hotelling's $T^2$.
#### Hypotheses
*   **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):** $\mathbf{\mu}_1 = \mathbf{\mu}_2$ (The mean vectors of the two populations are equal).
*   **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$):** $\mathbf{\mu}_1 \neq \mathbf{\mu}_2$ (The mean vectors differ in at least one dimension).

#### Test Statistic (Welch-Satterthwaite Extension)
The multivariate test statistic for unequal covariances is defined as:

$$T_{W}^2 = (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \left( \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2} \right)^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)$$

Where:
*   $\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1, \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2$: Sample mean vectors of the two groups.
*   $\mathbf{S}_1, \mathbf{S}_2$: Sample covariance matrices.
*   $n_1, n_2$: Sample sizes.

#### Distribution Approximation
Unlike the standard Hotelling's $T^2$ which follows an $F$-distribution, under $\mathbf{\Sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\Sigma}_2$, the statistic $T_{W}^2$ is approximated by a scaled $F$-distribution or a $\chi^2$ distribution depending on the sample size.

For large samples:
$$T_{W}^2 \sim \chi^2_p$$
where $p$ is the number of variables (dimensions).

For smaller samples, we use the **Yao (1965) approximation** to find the effective degrees of freedom ($v$):
$$v = \frac{\left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2}{\frac{1}{n_1-1} \left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2 + \frac{1}{n_2-1} \left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2} \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2}$$
where $\mathbf{W} = \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2}$.

#### Implementation Note (Python/R)
In practice, calculating the effective degrees of freedom manually is tedious. 
*   **Python:** Use `spicy.stats` for univariate, but for multivariate, you typically use the `Hotelling` package or manually implement the Welch-James correction.
*   **R:** The `biotools` or `WRS2` packages provide functions like `walrus` or custom Welch-type $T^2$ tests.

#### Summary Table
| Feature | Standard Hotelling's $T^2$ | Welch-James / Yao Test |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assumption** | $\mathbf{\Sigma}_1 = \mathbf{\Sigma}_2$ | $\mathbf{\Sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\Sigma}_2$ |
| **Covariance** | Pooled ($\mathbf{S}_{pl}$) | Unpooled ($\frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2}$) |
| **Robustness** | Low (if $n_1 \neq n_2$) | High |

## I.2 Principles of Microeconomics:
### 1. Overview
Microeconomics is the "Logic of the Firm". It explains how supply, demand, and price elasticity affect a company's bottom line. As a BA, you will use this to perform Cost-Benefit Analysis and Market Analysis.
### Core concepts (must remember):
- `Supply & Demand:` Understand how market forces determine price and quantity. This helps you predict how external changes affect a business.
- `Opportunity Cost:` The value of the next best alternative.
- `Marginal Analysis:` Decision-making based on the "next unit." A business should only proceed if the `Marginal Benefit > Marginal Cost`.

### 2. Application: Pricing strategy, competitor analysis, and cost-benefit analysis.
I would suggest a different approach to learning: instead of only memorizing formulas, try to remember how to visualize and draw the graphs behind them. If you can visualize the shift in supply or demand curves, you understand the logic.  
Also, focus on `Mankiw’s 10 Principles`. They provide a framework that applies to almost every business scenario.  
Finally, apply concepts to `daily life`. For example, why do grab prices surge during rain? (Answer: Demand shifts right, Supply shifts left).

### 3. Recommend sources:
- `"Naked Economics: Undressing the Dismal Science"` by `Charles Wheelan`. Yeah, I know, Charles Wheelan, again. This is the single most productive author in terms of breaking complex concepts into humorous and beginner-friendly ones.
This is widely considered the `"gold standard"` for people who want to understand economic logic through `wit and storytelling` rather than equations and complex graphs.
- `"Cartoon Introduction to Economics: Volume 1 (Microeconomics)"` by `Grady Klein and Yoram Bauman`: Yeah, this is literally a comic book. Yoram Bauman is a "stand-up economist," so the `humor` is top-tier, but the technical definitions are surprisingly accurate and easy to memorize.
