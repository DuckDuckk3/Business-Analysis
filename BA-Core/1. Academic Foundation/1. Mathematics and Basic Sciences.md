## I.1 Probability and Statistics:
### 1. Overview:
This is, undoubtedly, the `most important` subject in the entire pillar. Business Analysis is the art of `reducing uncertainty` to make `better decisions`. Without statistics, your perspective is just an opinion. You need this for data cleaning, hypothesis testing (A/B testing), and predictive modeling.
### Especially `hypothesis testing`, cuz it's a part of every business model. You'll also need probability to process raw data.  

For example, an e-commerce company wants to test whether a new checkout page increases the purchase rate.
They formulate the hypotheses:
- **Null hypothesis (H₀):** The new checkout page does not change the conversion rate.
- **Alternative hypothesis (H₁):** The new checkout page increases the conversion rate.  
The company runs an A/B test:

| Group | Visitors | Purchases | Conversion Rate |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Old page (A) | 10,000 | 420 | 4.2% |
| New page (B) | 10,000 | 510 | 5.1% |

After performing a statistical hypothesis test:
- significance level: α = 0.05
- p-value = 0.012
Because:
- p-value (0.012) < α (0.05) the company rejects the null hypothesis and concludes that the new checkout page significantly improves conversions.
  
This is hypothesis testing in business:  
`Companies` do `not` rely only on `“the numbers look bigger"`, but use `statistical evidence` (p-value, significance level, null hypothesis) to determine whether the improvement is likely real or just random chance.

### 2. Application: Predicting customer churn, A/B testing marketing campaigns, and quality control.
### 3. Recommend source: 
Since the knowledge taught in this subject at school is basically already sufficient, I think the things worth learning (which are not taught at school) are `hypothesis testing and multivariate random variables`, especially `under the assumption of unequal variances`.
### Multivariate Hypothesis Testing with Unequal Covariance Matrices

In multivariate analysis, when comparing the mean vectors of two independent groups ($\mathbf{\mu}_1$ and $\mathbf{\mu}_2$) without assuming equal covariance matrices ($\mathbf{\sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\sigma}_2$), we face the multivariate version of the **Behrens-Fisher problem**. 

The standard Hotelling's $T^2$ test is sensitive to violations of the homogeneity of covariance matrices. Instead, we use **Welch–James (WJ) Test** or a modified version of Hotelling's $T^2$.
#### Hypotheses
*   **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):** $\mathbf{\mu}_1 = \mathbf{\mu}_2$ (The mean vectors of the two populations are equal).
*   **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$):** $\mathbf{\mu}_1 \neq \mathbf{\mu}_2$ (The mean vectors differ in at least one dimension).

#### Test Statistic (Welch-Satterthwaite Extension)
The multivariate test statistic for unequal covariances is defined as:

$$T_{W}^2 = (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \left( \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2} \right)^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)$$

Where:
*   $\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1, \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2$: Sample mean vectors of the two groups.
*   $\mathbf{S}_1, \mathbf{S}_2$: Sample covariance matrices.
*   $n_1, n_2$: Sample sizes.

#### Distribution Approximation
Unlike the standard Hotelling's $T^2$ which follows an $F$-distribution, under $\mathbf{\sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\sigma}_2$, the statistic $\mathrm{T}_W^2$ is approximated by a scaled $F$-distribution or a $\chi^2$ distribution depending on the sample size.

For large samples:
$$T_{W}^2 \sim \chi^2_p$$
where $p$ is the number of variables (dimensions).

For smaller samples, we use the **Yao (1965) approximation** to find the effective degrees of freedom ($v$):
$$v = \frac{\left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2}{\frac{1}{n_1-1} \left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2 + \frac{1}{n_2-1} \left[ (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2)^T \mathbf{W}^{-1} \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2} \mathbf{W}^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{x}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{x}}_2) \right]^2}$$
where $\mathbf{W} = \frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2}$.

#### Implementation Note (Python/R)
In practice, calculating the effective degrees of freedom manually is tedious. 
*   **Python:** Use `spicy.stats` for univariate, but for multivariate, you typically use the `Hotelling` package or manually implement the Welch-James correction.
*   **R:** The `biotools` or `WRS2` packages provide functions like `walrus` or custom Welch-type $T^2$ tests.

#### Summary Table
| Feature | Standard Hotelling's $T^2$ | Welch-James / Yao Test |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assumption** | $\mathbf{\sigma}_1 = \mathbf{\sigma}_2$ | $\mathbf{\sigma}_1 \neq \mathbf{\sigma}_2$ |
| **Covariance** | Pooled ($\mathbf{S}_{pl}$) | Unpooled ($\frac{\mathbf{S}_1}{n_1} + \frac{\mathbf{S}_2}{n_2}$) |
| **Robustness** | Low (if $n_1 \neq n_2$) | High |

## I.2 Principles of Microeconomics:
### 1. Overview
Microeconomics is the "Logic of the Firm". It explains how supply, demand, and price elasticity affect a company's bottom line. As a BA, you will use this to perform Cost-Benefit Analysis and Market Analysis.
### Core concepts (must remember):
- `Supply & Demand:` Understand how market forces determine price and quantity. This helps you predict how external changes affect a business.
- `Opportunity Cost:` The value of the next best alternative.
- `Marginal Analysis:` Decision-making based on the "next unit." A business should only proceed if the `Marginal Benefit > Marginal Cost`.

### 2. Application: Pricing strategy, competitor analysis, and cost-benefit analysis.
I would suggest a different approach to learning: instead of only memorizing formulas, try to remember how to visualize and draw the graphs behind them. If you can visualize the shift in supply or demand curves, you understand the logic.  
Also, focus on `Mankiw’s 10 Principles`. They provide a framework that applies to almost every business scenario.  
Finally, apply concepts to `daily life`. For example, why do grab prices surge during rain? (Answer: Demand shifts right, Supply shifts left).

### 3. Recommend sources:
- `"Naked Economics: Undressing the Dismal Science"` by `Charles Wheelan`. Yeah, I know, Charles Wheelan, again. This is the single most productive author in terms of breaking complex concepts into humorous and beginner-friendly ones.
This is widely considered the `"gold standard"` for people who want to understand economic logic through `wit and storytelling` rather than equations and complex graphs.
- `"Cartoon Introduction to Economics: Volume 1 (Microeconomics)"` by `Grady Klein and Yoram Bauman`: Yeah, this is literally a comic book. Yoram Bauman is a "stand-up economist," so the `humor` is top-tier, but the technical definitions are surprisingly accurate and easy to memorize.

## II.1 Calculus I, III (Multivariable Calculus):
### 1. Overview:
While you might not solve triple integrals daily, `Calculus I, III` is about `Optimization`. In business, we are always trying to find the maximum profit or minimum cost given multiple constraints (labor, time, capital). Partial derivatives are the math behind optimization algorithms.  
Focus on the concept of `Gradients and Optimization`. Understand how changing one variable affects the whole system while others stay constant.  
- `Gradients:` These point in the direction of the steepest increase. In business, following the gradient helps you find the fastest path to maximum profit.
- `Lagrange Multipliers:` This is the "Holy Grail" for BA. It solves the problem: "Maximize Profit (Function A) given a limited Budget (Constraint B)."
### 2. Application: Profit maximization under multiple constraints (e.g., labor, material, and transport costs).
#### A. Profit Maximization (Lagrange Multipliers)
**Scenario:** A firm’s production depends on `Labor` ($L$) and `Capital` ($K$). The `production function` is $f(L, K)$. If Labor costs $w$ and Capital costs $r$, and the firm has a `fixed budget` $B$, we must solve:
$$\text{Maximize } f(L, K) \text{ subject to } wL + rK = B$$
By using `partial derivatives` to find where the `gradient of the production function` aligns with the `budget constraint`, you identify the most efficient allocation of resources.
#### B. Continuous Compounding (Differential Equations)
**Scenario:** `Calculating the growth of an investment` where interest is added constantly at every possible instant.
The rate of change of the balance ($A$) over time ($t$) is expressed as:
$$\frac{dA}{dt} = rA$$
Solving this `differential equation` leads to the continuous compounding formula:
$$A = Pe^{rt}$$
*   **BA Insight:** This is essential for calculating the **Time Value of Money (TVM)**, assessing long-term debt, or valuing financial derivatives in quantitative research.
### 3. Recommend sources:
- `Professor Leonard` (YouTube) for deep understanding.
- `"Fundamental Methods of Mathematical Economics" by Alpha C. Chiang`: Unlike pure math books, this explains Calculus specifically for business. It turns abstract symbols into "Cost," "Revenue," and "Utility." It is perfect for a BA mindset.

## II.2 Applied Mathematics in Business and Trade:
### 1. Overview:
This is the "Bridge" between pure mathematical theory and practical execution, providing the formulas for survival. It focuses on the time value of money, the mechanics of debt, and the logic of logistics. You will use these tools to build financial models, evaluate the feasibility of new ventures, and optimize supply chains.
### Key Concepts:
- `Time Value of Money (TVM):` $1 today is worth more than $1 tomorrow due to its potential earning capacity.
- `Annuities & Perpetuities:` Mathematical sequences used to calculate regular payments, such as loan amortizations, insurance premiums, or stock dividends.
- `Linear Programming (LP):` A method used to find the best outcome (such as maximum profit or lowest cost) in a mathematical model whose requirements are represented by linear relationships.
  
### 2. Application: Financial modeling and supply chain logistics.
Imagine a company is deciding whether to invest in a new `$1M software system`. The system will save the company $250,000 per year for 5 years. You must use `Net Present Value (NPV)` and `Internal Rate of Return (IRR)` calculations.  
You must discount future savings back to today's value using the formula:
    $$NPV = \sum \frac{R_t}{(1+i)^t} - \text{Initial Investment}$$  
    If $NPV > 0$, the project is mathematically viable.  
    
### 3. Recommend sources:
- `"Business Mathematics"` by `Gary Clendenen & Stanley Salzman`: A very practical, step-by-step guide to the math used in everyday business transactions (interest, payroll, taxes, and risk).
- `Investopedia (Quantitative Analysis Section)`: Excellent for quick, clear explanations of financial math concepts like CAGR, WACC, and NPV.

## III.1 Principles of Macroeconomics
### 1. Overview
If Microeconomics is the "Logic of the Firm," **Macroeconomics** is the "Logic of the Ocean." You might be a great sailor (micro), but if a hurricane (macro) hits, your boat is still going down. Macroeconomics helps you understand the **External Environment**—the massive forces like inflation, GDP growth, and unemployment that a company cannot control but must survive.
#### Key concepts:
- `GDP & Economic Cycles:` Understanding when the "party is starting" (Expansion) or when it's time to "hide the snacks" (Recession).
- `Inflation & Purchasing Power:` Why $100 today won't buy the same amount of cloud server space in three years.
- `Fiscal & Monetary Policy:` How the government and Central Banks "tinker" with the economy by changing taxes or interest rates.

### 2. Application: Strategic Planning & Forecasting
As a BA, you use Macro data for **PESTEL Analysis**. If the Central Bank raises interest rates, your company’s cost of borrowing for a new project goes up. You need to forecast whether a long-term project will remain profitable if the economy slows down.

### 3. Recommended Resources
- `"Naked Economics: Undressing the Dismal Science"` by `Charles Wheelan`: (Again, because it's that good). The chapters on the Federal Reserve and International Trade are famous for making "boring" policy sound like a high-stakes thriller.
- `Jacob Clifford - Macroeconomics (YouTube)`: He wears a GDP t-shirt and uses a high-energy, comedic style to explain everything. He makes Macro feel less like a lecture and more like a game show.
- `The "Planet Money" Podcast (NPR)`: These are short, witty stories that explain the "hidden" economic forces behind everything from the price of t-shirts to the global supply of semiconductors.

## III.2 Mathematical Economics & General Economic Informatics
### 1. Overview
This is where the "Philosophy" of economics meets the "Hard Power" of mathematics and software. **Mathematical Economics** takes the theories you've learned and turns them into rigorous equations. **Economic Informatics** then takes those equations and puts them into a computer to simulate reality. It’s essentially "SimCity" for professional analysts.  
These subjects contains important concepts: Game theory, Input-Output analysis and `ERP & Simulation` (especially important for your semi-tech desiration).

### 2. Application: Advanced Modeling
You will use these skills to build `Predictive Models`. Instead of just saying "we might lose money," you build a simulation that says: *"There is a 15% probability of a loss if the exchange rate drops by 2 points."* This is the bridge to becoming a `Quantitative Analyst` (I think it's a career that can fit you, since `algorithmic trading and quantitative trading` is becoming a `continuous trend`).

### 3. Recommended Resources
- `"The Art of Strategy" by Dixit & Nalebuff`: A witty and accessible introduction to **Game Theory**. It uses examples from pop culture and history to show how math can predict human (and corporate) behavior.
- `Marginal Revolution University (MRU.org):` Their videos on "Game Theory" and "Development Economics" are visually stunning and often include quirky, dry humor.
